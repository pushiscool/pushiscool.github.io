Unit 1 – Thinking Geographically & Development

Introducing Argentina

Argentina sits at the southern tip of South America between the Andes Mountains and the Atlantic Ocean. Its largest cities are Buenos Aires, Córdoba, Rosario, Mendoza and Salta.

Provinces

The nation is a federal republic made of 23 provinces and the autonomous city of Buenos Aires. Each province runs its own schools and police.

Choropleth Map

Darker shades mark areas with more people on the Pampas plain. Light shades in Patagonia show very few residents.

Cartogram

This map resizes provinces by population so Buenos Aires looks huge. Thin areas in the south show sparse settlement.

Proportional Symbols

Large circles point to busy airports that link the country. Small circles show towns with fewer flights.

Dot-Density Map

Each dot equals about 2 500 people on the ground. The empty south proves Patagonia is mostly open land.

Environmental Determinism

The mild Pampas climate supports wheat fields and cattle ranches. Local food and clothing grew from these farm traditions.

Possibilism

Farmers built canals to bring water to dry valleys in the west. These projects turned deserts into orchards and vineyards.

World-Systems

Argentina sells farm goods yet also makes cars and steel. This mix puts it in the semi-periphery group.

Key Metrics

The economy is worth about US$612 billion and the HDI is high. Literacy is almost universal at 99 percent.

Gender Inequality

The GII score is 0.287 which shows moderate gaps. Women now hold roughly forty percent of seats in congress.

Rostow Stage

The nation fits Rostow’s Drive-to-Maturity stage with growing industry. Farming still matters but services are rising fast.

Unit 2 – Population & Migration

Population & Density

The fertile Pampas holds most of the 46 million inhabitants. Mountains, deserts and cold southern lands remain thinly settled.

Vital Rates

Both birth and death rates are low, showing an ageing society. The total fertility rate is only 1.36 babies per woman.

Population Pyramid

The pyramid sides are nearly straight which means slow growth. Elderly groups are expanding while child groups shrink.

Demographic Transition

The country sits in Stage 4 with low births and deaths. Life expectancy is high and natural increase is tiny.

Migration

Many Bolivians and Paraguayans move in for work. Some Argentines leave for Spain and the United States.

Push & Pull

Inflation and job shortages push people away. Higher wages and family links in richer countries pull them abroad.

Unit 3 – Culture

Folk Culture

Empanadas and mate tea are eaten daily in many homes. Gauchos wear loose bombacha pants and ride horses on the plains.

Pop Culture

Football dominates TV, music and street talk. Boca Juniors games fill the famous Bombonera stadium with loud fans.

Language

Spanish is the official language and almost everyone speaks it. The local accent uses a unique “sh” sound in words like “calle.”

Religion

About two-thirds of people identify as Catholic and celebrate Mass. Evangelical churches and secular beliefs are growing fast.

Unit 4 – Political Geography

Government

Argentina is a federal presidential republic with three branches. Citizens elect the president every four years by popular vote.

Ethnicity

Most people have Spanish or Italian ancestry from past immigration waves. Indigenous Mapuche and Qom communities live mainly in the north and south.

Colonial Legacy

Spain ruled the Viceroyalty of Río de la Plata until 1816. Colonial trade paths later became modern rail and road lines.

Antecedent Boundary

The 1881 treaty fixed the border along the Andes before heavy settlement. Today most towns sit east of this high mountain line.

Irredentism

Argentina claims the Falkland Islands which Britain calls the Malvinas. The dispute flared into war in 1982 and remains unresolved.

Supranationalism

Argentina helped create MERCOSUR in 1991 to lower trade barriers. The bloc boosts exports to Brazil, Paraguay and Uruguay.

Unit 5 – Agriculture

Agricultural Land

About half of Argentina’s land is cropland or pasture. The flat Pampas has deep soil perfect for grain and cattle.

Main Crops

Soybeans, corn and wheat lead farm exports each year. High-quality beef is a key national brand.

Mechanisation

High tractor numbers show large, modern farms that use machines. Small family farms are less common here than in other regions.

Farm Labour

Farming employs under ten percent of workers, so most jobs are urban. This low share means the economy is more industrial and service based.

Unit 6 – Cities & Urban Land Use

Megacity

Greater Buenos Aires hosts around fifteen million residents in its metro area. It stretches 60 km from the historic center to outer suburbs.

Rank-Size

Buenos Aires is many times larger than Córdoba or Rosario, making it a primate city. This imbalance pulls jobs and culture toward the capital.

World City Links

Ezeiza Airport sends flights to Europe, North America and Asia. International firms choose Buenos Aires for its skilled labor and time zone.

Land-Use Model

The historic CBD sits by the port and Plaza de Mayo. Rail lines run outward with industry while new suburbs ring the edge.

Unit 7 – Industrialization

GDP by Sector

Services make up fifty-five percent of GDP, led by finance and tourism. Industry provides one-quarter while farming adds the rest.

Energy Corridor

Vaca Muerta holds huge shale oil and gas reserves in Neuquén Province. New pipelines aim to ship this energy to ports and neighbors.

Trade Bloc

MERCOSUR lets goods move tariff-free among members which helps factories sell regionally. Brazil is Argentina’s top buyer.

Auto Plants

Toyota builds Hilux trucks and SW4 SUVs at its Zárate plant. Vehicles ship to twenty Latin American markets.
